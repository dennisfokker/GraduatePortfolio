CAR INVENTOR RACE

Car inventor race is a game about racing your new car invention to the exposition, which you are late for.
Rush through the city in your car whilst swerving around traffic, avoiding pedestrians and crashing through pesky wooden blockades.

CONCEPT

You’ve just created a car with a new steering design: leaning. One player controls the car in VR, leaning left and right to evade all the obstacles in the car’s path. A second player can view the driver’s HMD view on a screen and will control the speed, able to go throttle from fast to slow, but never truly stop (you are late, after all).

While the path the players have to take is linear, you do have full control over the car (unlike games like aircycle and balloon escape).

DEVELOPMENT

dynamic obstacles

Dynamic obstacles are obstacles that move around in various ways. At first the idea was to make the dynamic obstacle actor contain a static mesh itself, but this later changed to simply contain a reference to a different actor. This allows for easier custom behaviour in said actor (such as sounds and animations).

A dynamic obstacle contains 4 different movement types: follow spline by time, follow spline by speed (later addition), translate by time and animate object.

Most movement types should speak for themselves: there’s a spline that can be adjusted in the level that the actor will be moved along. Translation means a simple relative location and/or rotation change. Object animation doesn’t really do anything: it simply calls an interface function on the moved actor, which can implement its own animation logic (animations can optionally be ran with every movement type).

Aside from this movement, looping has been implemented too. Later during development some dynamic obstacles weren’t one-time triggers anymore but had to constantly move around (such as a crane idly rotating back and forth).

The looping types are: no looping, reverse looping (do the same movement backwards) and teleport looping (jump back to your starting position and start moving again).

score

(Almost) all PROTO games provide the player with some score value at the end of the game. To make all game scores more connected, they all follow a certain score format, something we called “score standardisation”: a “decent”-rated player should get roughly the same score in every game.

The document that outlines all the score standardisation values/calculations can be found here .

This game is literally a race against the clock, thus the time you have left is a logical variable for this game’s score. To provide a fair score for people who are unable to reach the destination, the distance travelled (how close are you at the destination, based on your location on a spline) is an addition to the total score.

The ratio variable makes it so that every percent of distance travelled (to the destination) counts equal to a percent of time the player has left.

The values were chosen by letting developers (experienced) and new players give the game a go. A player who has played the game multiple times or happens to be naturally very good at the game should be able to have 3 minutes left on the clock, whilst we see that every player should be able to reach at least about halfway to the destination.
